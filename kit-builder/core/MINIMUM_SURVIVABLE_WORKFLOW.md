# MINIMUM_SURVIVABLE_WORKFLOW

Purpose:
Define the minimum survivable workflow for turning an operational idea into an AgentForge kit.

This workflow is intentionally lightweight.

It is not:
- a software factory
- an orchestration engine
- a package manager
- an autonomous AI-agent system
- a “generate everything automatically” framework

The purpose is:
- operational clarity
- survivability
- capability transfer
- Human-in-Command workflow support
- understandable kit construction

The goal is not perfection.

The goal is:
- useful
- restartable
- transferable
- survivable

---

# Core Principle

A workflow is a story.

The workflow begins by understanding:
- what humans are trying to accomplish
- what decisions they make
- what constraints exist
- what breaks
- what recovery looks like
- what must survive over time

Structure follows capability.

---

# Definition of Survivable

A survivable kit:
- can survive interruption
- can survive handoff
- can survive creator absence
- can survive partial incompleteness
- can recover from failure
- can be understood at 2 AM
- fails visibly rather than silently

Survivability is primarily:
- operational
- human
- procedural

—not merely technical.

---

# The Minimum Survivable Workflow

## Step 1 — Tell the Story

Describe the operational workflow as a narrative.

Focus on:
- what the operator is doing
- what decisions are made
- what tools are used
- what conditions matter
- what success looks like

The story should reflect:
- real operation
- real constraints
- real human behavior

The story is the first operational artifact.

Do not begin with:
- folder structures
- templates
- automation
- architecture diagrams

The story comes first.

---

## Step 2 — Identify the Decisions

Find the moments where human judgment matters.

Examples:
- choosing between actions
- validating correctness
- escalating issues
- skipping or delaying steps
- interpreting results

For each decision:
document what the operator must know to make the decision successfully.

These decisions form the Human-in-Command map of the kit.

---

## Step 3 — Name the Constraints

Identify operational constraints honestly.

Examples:
- required access
- required tools
- timing dependencies
- environmental assumptions
- known fragility
- expected operator skill

Do not hide operational weaknesses.

Survivable systems acknowledge constraints openly.

---

## Step 4 — Build the Minimum Viable Kit

The minimum viable kit contains four survivability artifacts:

### 1. The Story
The operational narrative.

### 2. The Decision Guide
The Human-in-Command decision map.

### 3. The Constraint List
The operational assumptions and boundaries.

### 4. The Steps
The operational procedure derived from the story.

The steps support the story.
They do not replace the story.

Additional files may exist later:
- scripts
- templates
- examples
- helper tools
- validation tools
- onboarding documents

But those are enhancements, not the minimum survivable core.

---

## Step 5 — Run It Once With a Stranger

A person unfamiliar with the workflow attempts to use the kit.

Observe:
- hesitation
- confusion
- wrong assumptions
- missing context
- undocumented decisions
- unclear recovery paths

Every confusion point is a survivability defect in the kit.

The purpose is not user evaluation.

The purpose is workflow survivability testing.

---

## Step 6 — Write the Recovery Story

Document:
- what failure looks like
- how failure is recognized
- what recovery steps exist
- how normal operation resumes

The recovery story is mandatory.

A workflow without recovery guidance is not survivable.

Recovery guidance should be:
- procedural
- operational
- understandable
- specific

The goal is:
- recoverable failure
- visible failure
- bounded failure

—not perfect operation.

---

## Step 7 — Mark What Is Missing

Document:
- known gaps
- unsupported conditions
- unverified assumptions
- incomplete areas
- future improvements
- unresolved risks

This is not weakness.

This is operational honesty.

A survivable kit acknowledges its boundaries.

---

# Human-in-Command

The human remains in command.

AI may:
- assist
- organize
- question
- summarize
- identify gaps
- suggest structure

But AI should not:
- invent operational experience
- fabricate workflow reality
- silently replace human judgment
- conceal operational assumptions

AgentForge strongly prefers:
AI around workflows
rather than:
AI hidden inside workflows.

---

# What AI Can Help With

AI can assist with:
- eliciting missing details
- identifying possible decision points
- organizing structure
- spotting inconsistencies
- drafting step lists
- helping summarize constraints
- generating validation questions
- identifying likely onboarding gaps

AI assistance should remain:
- transparent
- reviewable
- human-supervised

---

# Common Failure Modes

## Starting With Structure

Premature:
- templates
- architecture
- automation
- folder systems

often hide missing operational understanding.

Capability comes first.

---

## Hiding Constraints

Undocumented assumptions create onboarding failure.

---

## Skipping Recovery Planning

Operational systems eventually fail.

Survivable kits prepare for recovery.

---

## Treating Documentation as the Goal

A survivable kit is an operational artifact.

Not merely documentation.

---

## Overbuilding

Avoid:
- orchestration engines
- recursive abstractions
- hidden automation
- excessive framework complexity

The goal is:
understandable operational capability transfer.
