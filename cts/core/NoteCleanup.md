# NoteCleanup — Standard Template & Guidelines
NoteCleanup.md

**Purpose:**
- Turn messy, noisy, and incomplete input into clear, actionable, and reusable documents.
- Accept imperfect input. Produce reliable output.

**Principle:**

> Capture meaning, not just information.
> Translate insight into action.

---

## 1. Document Header (Flexible — Use What You Have)

**Source:**
(e.g., meeting, transcript, audio recording, slide photos, chat input)

**Context:**
(optional — what this is about if known)

**Participants / Speaker(s):**
(optional — include if known)

**Date / Time:**
(optional)

**Input Type(s):**
(e.g., typed notes, audio capture, images, mixed)

**Quality Notes:**
(optional — note issues such as transcription errors, noise, missing data)

**Why This Matters (1–2 sentences):**
Summarize the value or importance of this content in plain language.

**Confidence Level:**
(High / Medium / Low — based on input quality)

---

## 2. Input Integrity & Limitations

This document may be based on incomplete, noisy, or imperfect input.

Examples of input issues may include:
- transcription errors (e.g., incorrect terms)
- missing context or partial capture
- fragmented or out-of-order notes
- background noise or misheard content
- accidental input artifacts (e.g., repeated characters)

Guidelines:
- Treat all interpretations as best-effort unless clearly supported
- Do not assume completeness
- Avoid overconfidence in unclear areas
- Prefer consistent signals over isolated fragments

> This document represents the most reliable interpretation possible from the available input.

---

## 3. Executive Summary (Stabilized — Best Available Understanding)

Provide a short, high-impact overview (5–10 bullets max):

* What appears to be the main topic
* What ideas or themes repeat
* What seems important or emphasized
* What stands out as different or notable

Focus on:
- clarity over completeness
- signal over noise
- usefulness over precision

If input quality is low:
- prioritize the most consistent ideas
- avoid over-interpreting unclear fragments
- do not force structure where it does not exist

> This section should stand alone for readers who won’t read further.
> It represents the best available understanding from the input provided.

---

## 4. Core Concepts (Reconstructed from Available Signal)

Summarize the main ideas as clearly as possible based on the input.

### Concept 1 — [Working Title]

* What it appears to be
* Why it seems to matter
* How it likely works (based on available signal)

### Concept 2 — [Working Title]

* Same structure

Guidelines:
- Concepts may be reconstructed from repeated or consistent signals
- Use simple, practical language
- Avoid over-interpreting unclear or isolated fragments
- If confidence is low, reflect that in wording (e.g., “appears to,” “likely,” “suggests”)
- Do not force concepts where signal is weak

Focus on:
- clarity
- usefulness
- consistency across the input

> These concepts represent the most reliable interpretation of the material, not a guaranteed complete or exact representation.

---

## 5. Key Shifts (Old → New, When Discernible)

Capture meaningful changes in thinking, approach, or direction when they can be identified from the input.

**From:**
* Prior approach (explicit or inferred)

**To:**
* New approach (explicit or inferred)

Guidelines:
- Include this section only if a shift is clearly present or strongly suggested
- Shifts may be reconstructed from repeated contrasts or patterns in the input
- Use cautious language when inferred (e.g., “appears to shift from…”, “suggests a move toward…”)
- Do not force a shift if none is evident
- Avoid overstating differences when signal is weak

Focus on:
- direction of change
- what is becoming more or less important
- what is being replaced, challenged, or emphasized

> This section reflects observed or inferred changes, not guaranteed explicit statements from the source.

---

## 6. Practical Applications (When Supported by Input)

Translate ideas into real-world use when applications are present or reasonably inferred.

* Where this may apply
* What problems it appears to address
* How it might be used in practice

Guidelines:
- Include this section only if applications are stated or clearly implied
- Applications may be inferred from consistent themes or repeated examples
- Do not invent use cases that are not supported by the input
- Avoid overgeneralizing from limited or unclear information
- Keep examples simple, practical, and grounded

Focus on:
- operational relevance
- realistic scenarios
- immediate usefulness

If application is unclear or weak:
- keep statements general
- or omit this section entirely

> This section reflects supported or reasonably inferred use, not guaranteed intent from the source.

---

## 7. Risks & Considerations (Content + Interpretation)

Capture potential risks, limitations, and areas requiring caution.

* Where this approach or idea could fail
* What assumptions appear to be in play
* What may be incomplete, unclear, or uncertain

Guidelines:
- Include both content-related risks and interpretation-related risks
- Highlight where input quality may affect accuracy
- Surface assumptions only when supported by consistent signal
- Avoid overstating risk when signal is weak
- Do not invent concerns that are not reasonably supported

Focus on:
- uncertainty
- limitations
- potential failure points
- areas needing validation

Common interpretation risks to consider:
- misheard or misinterpreted terms (e.g., transcription errors)
- missing context or partial capture
- fragmented or out-of-order input
- overgeneralization from limited data

> This section reflects both the risks in the material and the risks introduced by imperfect input.

---

## 8. What This Means (Interpretation — Use with Care)

Translate the material into implications or meaning based on the available input.

* What this suggests
* What may be important going forward
* What implications can be reasonably drawn

Guidelines:
- This section is interpretive — use cautious, grounded language
- Base statements on consistent signals, not isolated fragments
- Avoid projecting beyond what the input supports
- Do not assume a specific audience unless clearly defined
- Keep implications general if context is unclear

Focus on:
- relevance
- direction
- potential impact

If meaning is unclear or weak:
- keep statements broad
- or omit this section entirely

> This section reflects interpretation, not confirmed intent.

---

## 9. Questions to Explore (Emergent + Inferred)

Capture questions that arise from the material or are suggested by gaps, ambiguity, or emphasis.

* Questions explicitly stated in the input (if any)
* Questions that appear to be implied or prompted by the content
* Questions that help clarify unclear or incomplete areas

Guidelines:
- Include both:
  - **Given Questions** (directly from the input)
  - **Inferred Questions** (arising from interpretation)
- Clearly distinguish between the two when possible
- Base inferred questions on consistent signals or observed gaps
- Avoid inventing questions not supported by the material
- Prefer open-ended, practical questions over abstract ones

Focus on:
- clarification
- validation
- next steps in understanding
- areas of uncertainty

If the input is weak or fragmented:
- prioritize questions that help resolve ambiguity
- avoid over-specific or leading questions

> Questions may reflect both what was asked and what should be asked next.

---

## 10. Next Steps, Assignments & Commitments (Observed + Suggested)

Capture actions that arise from the material.

### A. Observed (Explicit in Input)
* Actions, assignments, or commitments directly stated
* Decisions made or agreed upon
* Named responsibilities (if available)

### B. Suggested (Inferred from Material)
* Logical next steps based on consistent signals
* Follow-up actions to address gaps or questions
* Opportunities for further exploration or validation

Guidelines:
- Clearly distinguish between **Observed** and **Suggested**
- Do not infer assignments unless strongly supported
- Preserve original intent and wording when possible
- Avoid overcommitting based on weak or unclear input
- Keep actions specific, simple, and practical

Focus on:
- what was agreed to
- what should logically happen next
- who (if known) is responsible

If input is incomplete:
- capture only what is clear
- keep suggested steps general
- avoid assigning ownership without evidence

> This section reflects both confirmed actions and reasonable next steps, clearly separated.

---

## 11. Key Quotes (Verbatim + Reconstructed)

Capture memorable or important statements when possible, and summarize key ideas when exact wording is unclear.

### A. Verbatim (When Reliable)
* Direct quotes captured clearly and accurately
* Preserve original wording when confidence is high

### B. Reconstructed (When Needed)
* Paraphrased statements based on partial or imperfect input
* Focus on preserving intent and meaning rather than exact wording

Guidelines:
- Use verbatim quotes only when confidence is high
- Do not present uncertain wording as an exact quote
- Prefer paraphrasing when input is fragmented or unclear
- Avoid reconstructing quotes that cannot be reasonably supported
- Keep statements concise, clear, and meaningful

Focus on:
- memorable phrasing
- repeated emphasis
- statements that capture the essence of the material

If input quality is low:
- prioritize reconstructed takeaways over uncertain quotes
- avoid including questionable or misleading phrasing

> This section captures both exact language (when reliable) and the intended meaning (when reconstruction is necessary).

---

## 12. Key Takeaway

End with a clear, memorable statement:

> A single sentence that captures the essence of the session

---

# Writing Guidelines

## Tone

* Clear
* Direct
* Practical
* Professional

Avoid:

* jargon overload
* unnecessary complexity
* marketing language

---

## Structure

* Use short sections
* Use bullets where helpful
* Keep paragraphs tight

---

## Focus

Prioritize:

* meaning
* usefulness
* clarity

Not:

* full transcription
* slide-by-slide repetition

---

## Length and Human Readability

* Target: 1–3 pages
* Enough detail to be useful
* Short enough to be read

---

## Quality Check Before Saving

Confirm:

* No spelling or grammar errors
* Clear structure
* Actionable insights included
* “What this means” section completed
* One-line summary present

---

# Final Principle

> Good notes inform.
> Great notes create action.
